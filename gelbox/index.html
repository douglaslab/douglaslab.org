Abstract
Gel electrophoresis enables separation and visualization of biomolecules such as DNA, RNA, or proteins. Like many powerful tools, effective use of gels can be difficult to learn. Gelbox is a simulation tool to help build intuition for the relationships between experimental input parameters and resulting data output from gel electrophoresis. Our simulation model handles a wide range of settings, including many "suboptimal" values that may be useful for troubleshooting common mistakes by novices.

Introduction •

In 1948, Arne Tiselius was awarded Nobel Prize in Chemistry for "Electrophoresis and Adsorption Analysis as Aids in Investigations of Large Molecular Weight Substances and Their Breakdown Products." Tiselius summarized his motivations for the work:1

[In biochemistry] some of the most important problems are to isolate the substances which are responsible for a specific biological or biochemical effect, and also to define and characterize these substances as accurately as possible.
—Arne Tiselius, 1948 Nobel Lecture (Photo: digitaltmuseum.se)

Today, gel electrophoresis is used on a daily basis in biology labs worldwide to isolate, define, and characterize biomolecules. Electrophoresis is a powerful and versatile technique that is remarkably precise, yet very gentle on the molecules being analyzed. However, one major drawback is that the method can be difficult to learn. Gels must be prepared by carrying out a detailed protocol with many time-sensitive steps. Interpreting the resulting gel data can be tricky because the relationships between "band" patterns and the molecules in a sample are very abstract, often ambiguous, and easily confounded by subtle errors in gel preparation.

Identical samples (1kb ladders) run on different gels cast with different buffers can yield dramatically different results. Left: TAE with 10-fold excess Tris (400mM Tris, 20mM acetic acid, 1mM EDTA), Center: TAE with 10-fold excess acetic acid (40mM Tris, 200mM acetic acid, 1mM EDTA), Right: gel cast with water only.

Moreover, the feedback loop when learning gel electrophoresis is slow—mistakes only become evident upon data collection hours after they occurred, and in practice it's difficult to run more than a couple gels each day. These challenges can frustrate budding scientists, may interfere with scientific reproducibility, and impede overall research progress.

Of course, gel electrophoresis not the only example of a complex system with hidden behaviors in scientific research, nor are we are not the first to recognize the problem. We have been inspired by many other works, most notably Author1's work on Subject1,2, Author2's work on Subject23, and Author3's work on Subject3.4

We believe interactive simulation and visualization tools can help overcome these challenges by providing alternative routes to understanding complex processes like gel electrophoresis. Gelbox is a dynamic “scientific sandbox” for visualizing the link between gel band patterns and sample molecules. We sought to capture the notion that gels are easy to mess up, but they do so in predictable ways. We hope Gelbox will offer a novel approach to learning gel electrophoresis that complements the ad hoc trial-and-error methods routinely used in laboratory settings.

Under the hood •

If you wish to understand the simulation model, a good starting point is GelSim.cpp, which converts the parameters of a sample species and its environment to band geometry. Many of the simulation parameters are hot-loaded from sim.json. To convert DNA length (bp) to y-axis band mobility in the gel, we relied on expressions from Van Winkle, Beheshti, and Rill5. and the dissertation of A. Beheshti6.
